10 Common Semi Truck Brake Problems
Quick Answer
The most common semi truck brake problems include air pressure loss, worn brake pads/shoes, brake fade, out-of-adjustment slack adjusters, glazed drums, cracked rotors, seized calipers, ABS sensor failures, brake imbalance, and contaminated brake fluid or air lines. Most are preventable with routine inspection and proper brake service intervals.

What Are Semi Truck Brake Problems?
Semi truck brake problems refer to any mechanical, hydraulic, or pneumatic failure that reduces the stopping power of a commercial vehicle weighing up to 80,000 lbs loaded. Because commercial trucks rely on air brake systems — not hydraulic systems like passenger cars — the failure modes are distinct and often more dangerous if ignored.
Federal Motor Carrier Safety Administration (FMCSA) data consistently shows brake-related issues as the leading mechanical cause of large truck crashes. Understanding the ten most common failures is essential for every driver, owner-operator, and fleet safety manager.







The 10 Most Common Semi Truck Brake Problems

Problem 01
Low or Lost Air Pressure
High Risk   Air System

Air pressure below 60 PSI triggers the low-pressure warning light. Below 45 PSI, spring brakes automatically apply — a safety mechanism that can lock up a moving truck.
Symptoms: warning buzzer, slow pressure buildup, brakes dragging
Causes: air leaks in lines/fittings, failed compressor, leaking tanks
Fix:
Soap-test all air lines and fittings. Replace leaking components. Check compressor output — it should build from 50 to 90 PSI in under 3 minutes.

Problem 02
Worn Brake Pads or Brake Shoes
Out-of-Service Risk Friction Material

FMCSA sets the minimum lining thickness at 1/4 inch for steering axles and 1/16 inch for other axles. Below these thresholds, the truck is placed out of service at any DOT inspection.
Symptoms: grinding noise, longer stopping distance, pulling to one side
Causes: normal wear, improper adjustment, running brakes hot on grades
Fix:
Replace brake shoes or pads as a complete axle set. Inspect drums and rotors for scoring at the same time — worn linings accelerate drum damage.

Problem 03
Out-of-Adjustment Slack Adjusters
Common OOS Violation Adjustment

Slack adjusters control the distance brake shoes travel before contacting the drum. Even automatic slack adjusters (ASAs) fail and must be checked manually — a common misconception is that ASAs never need inspection.
Symptoms: spongy pedal feel, uneven braking, long push-rod stroke
Causes: worn ASA pawl, manual adjusters backed off, improper initial setup
Fix:
Check push-rod stroke with brakes applied at 90 PSI. Stroke must not exceed limits set in FMCSA §393.47. Replace worn automatic slack adjusters — do not manually re-adjust an ASA repeatedly as a fix.

Problem 04
Brake Fade on Descents
Heat-RelatedMountain Routes

Brake fade occurs when sustained friction heat vaporizes the brake lining material, creating a gas layer between shoe and drum. The brakes feel normal but produce almost no stopping force — a terrifying and dangerous scenario on mountain grades.
Symptoms: pedal feels firm but stopping power disappears
Causes: riding brakes on long descents, overloaded trailer, wrong gear selection
Fix:
Downshift before grades — use engine braking as the primary retarder. Apply brakes in short firm applications, not sustained dragging. Allow cooling every 6–8 miles on steep routes.

Problem 05
Glazed Brake Drums or Rotors
Surface Condition

Glazing creates a hardened, mirror-like surface on the drum or rotor that reduces friction dramatically. It often develops when brakes run too hot repeatedly, or when new linings are installed without properly bedding them in.
Symptoms: reduced stopping power, squeal, hard pedal with little effect
Causes: heat cycling, improper break-in procedure, contamination
Fix:
Light glazing can be removed by scuffing with sandpaper or turning the drum on a lathe. Severe glazing or heat cracking requires drum/rotor replacement.

Problem 06
Cracked or Warped Brake Drums
Replace Immediately

Cracks typically originate at the cooling fins and extend inward. A cracked drum can fracture completely under hard braking — this is a catastrophic failure mode with no warning.
Symptoms: vibration under braking, visible heat discoloration, cracking sound
Causes: repeated thermal shock, oversized linings, improper drum diameter
Fix:
Any crack visible to the naked eye is grounds for immediate replacement. Do not regrind cracked drums. Measure drum diameter — drums worn beyond maximum allowable diameter (stamped on the drum) must be replaced.

Problem 07
Seized or Sticking Brake Caliper (Disc Brake Equipped Units)
Disc Systems  Heat

Air disc brakes are increasingly standard on tractors. A seized caliper causes one wheel to brake constantly, generating extreme heat, accelerating tire wear, and creating a pull that fights the steering wheel.
Symptoms: single wheel dramatically hotter than others, truck pulling, burning smell
Causes: corrosion, contaminated slides, brake dust buildup
Fix:
Use an infrared thermometer after a test drive — a seized caliper wheel runs 200°F+ hotter than its partner. Rebuild or replace caliper. Clean and lubricate guide pins with high-temp brake grease.


Problem 08
ABS Sensor Failure
Electronics Inspectable

A failed wheel speed sensor disables ABS on that corner, which can cause wheel lockup and loss of directional control on slick surfaces. The ABS warning lamp illuminates but the truck still has base braking — it's not a complete brake failure.
Symptoms: ABS lamp on dash, possible erratic brake behavior on wet roads
Causes: damaged wiring harness, corroded connector, failed tone ring
Fix:
Use a diagnostic scan tool to pull fault codes and identify the failed wheel position. Inspect tone ring for missing teeth and wiring for chafing near the axle. Replace sensor if resistance is outside spec (typically 900–2000 ohms).

Problem 09
Brake Imbalance (Truck vs. Trailer)
Jackknife Risk  Combination Vehicle
Brake timing imbalance between tractor and trailer is one of the leading causes of jackknifing. If trailer brakes apply first or harder than tractor brakes, the trailer pushes the tractor — especially on slippery surfaces.
Symptoms: trailer swings on braking, skid marks only on trailer tires
Causes: trailer relay valve out of spec, mismatched brake chamber sizes, air restriction
Fix:
Use a brake timing and force meter. The tractor should apply slightly before the trailer. Adjust or replace the trailer relay valve. Verify all brake chambers are the correct type and size for the axle.

Problem 10
Contaminated Air Lines (Water and Oil)
Preventable  Air System
Water in air lines freezes in winter, blocking valves and rendering brakes inoperative. Compressor oil contamination degrades rubber components including brake chamber diaphragms and valve seals throughout the system.
Symptoms: slow valve response in cold weather, oil residue at drain valves, swollen rubber components
Causes: failed air dryer, skipped drain maintenance, worn compressor rings
Fix:
Drain air tanks daily in high-humidity or cold conditions. Replace the air dryer desiccant cartridge annually. If oil contamination is found, replace the compressor and all downstream valves and chambers.

Pro Tip — Infrared Thermometer Test
After a 5-mile test drive with moderate braking, use an IR thermometer on each hub. All wheels should read within 50°F of each other. Any outlier indicates a dragging brake, seized caliper, or out-of-adjustment slack adjuster at that position.

When to Seek Professional Semi Truck Brake Service
Some brake issues can be handled in the yard with standard tools. But these situations require a qualified brake technician immediately:
Push-rod stroke exceeds FMCSA limits on any axle
Visible cracks in drums or rotors
Oil contamination confirmed in the air system
Brake imbalance causing trailer sway
Any brake issue flagged during a DOT inspection

Common Brake Inspection Mistakes to Avoid
Checking push-rod stroke with the engine off and brakes unapplied — always measure at 90 PSI with brakes applied
Replacing linings on one side of an axle only — always replace as a complete axle set
Assuming automatic slack adjusters never need inspection
Ignoring an illuminated ABS lamp because base braking feels normal
Draining only the primary tank and skipping the secondary and wet tank
Semi truck brake problems range from minor adjustments to catastrophic structural failures — but virtually all of them follow warning signs if you know what to look for. Regular pre-trip air pressure checks, post-drive wheel temperature scans, and proper inspection intervals catch 90% of issues before they become roadside emergencies or CSA violations.
For owner-operators and fleets, the math is simple: a proper brake service costs a fraction of a DOT out-of-service order, a cargo delay, or a preventable accident. Stay proactive, document every brake inspection, and work with certified technicians for anything beyond routine adjustment.
