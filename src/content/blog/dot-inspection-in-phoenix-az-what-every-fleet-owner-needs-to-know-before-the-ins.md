## What Inspectors Actually Check: The Level I Breakdown

A Level I inspection is the full walk-around, under the hood, under the truck, every critical system. These are the areas that generate the most violations on annual DOT inspection semi truck Arizona operators face:

**Brakes — the #1 out-of-service trigger**
- Brake adjustment out of spec (stroke exceeding limits for the chamber size)
- Cracked or oil-contaminated brake linings
- Inoperative or leaking air lines
- Worn brake drums beyond the discard thickness

If your S-cam slack adjusters haven't been touched since the last PM, get them checked. Automatic slack adjusters can mask wear — "automatic" doesn't mean "self-diagnosing."

**Tires**
- Tread depth below 4/32" on steer axles, 2/32" on drives and trailers
- Sidewall cuts, bulges, or repairs in the tread area on steer tires
- Mismatched sizes on a single axle

In Phoenix's summer heat, tire pressure fluctuates more than drivers realize. A tire that's at spec in the morning can be a problem after 200 miles of August blacktop.

**Lights and Electrical**
- Inoperative headlights, tail lights, brake lights, clearance lights
- Non-functional turn signals or hazard lights
- Trailer ABS fault light on

Corroded trailer connectors are the most common cause of intermittent lighting failures. A bad 7-way plug doesn't announce itself — it just shows up in front of an inspector at a chicken coop.

**Steering and Suspension**
- Excessive steering wheel free-play (more than 2" on a power-assisted unit)
- Cracked or broken leaf springs
- Air ride components that won't hold pressure

**Driver Docs**
Inspectors aren't just looking at the truck. CDL validity, medical certificate, ELD logs, hours of service records, registration, and insurance all get checked during a Level I. A clean truck with a missing medical cert is still an out-of-service event.

## Building a Pre-Inspection Checklist That Actually Works

Most trucks that fail an annual DOT inspection semi truck Arizona check have at least one issue the driver already knew about and didn't escalate. Fix that culture first.

Walk every unit through this before scheduling your inspection:

- **Air system:** Build to full pressure, shut the engine, watch the gauge. More than 2 psi drop per minute on a single vehicle is a problem.
- **Brake adjustment:** Manual check on each chamber stroke with a ruler. Don't assume the automatics are in spec.
- **Lights:** Full walk-around with a second person calling out each function. Don't forget the side markers and conspicuity tape condition.
- **Tires:** Inflate to spec and check for any irregular wear that suggests alignment or suspension problems.
- **Fluid leaks:** Oil, coolant, power steering fluid dripping anywhere near brakes is an automatic out-of-service.
- **Steering:** Check free play with wheels pointed straight ahead.
- **Documents:** Verify CDL, medical cert expiration, registration, and ELD data sync.

If you find brake or suspension issues during that walk, get them diagnosed before the inspection.
