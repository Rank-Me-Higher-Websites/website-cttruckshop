<h2 class="text-2xl font-bold text-gray-900 mb-4" style="border-left: 4px solid #DC2626; padding-left: 16px;">What Actually Breaks First in Arizona Heat</h2>
<div class="space-y-5 mb-10">
  <div class="rounded-xl p-5" style="background: #fef2f2; border-left: 4px solid #DC2626;">
    <h3 class="font-bold text-gray-900 mb-2">1. AC Compressor</h3>
    <p class="text-gray-700 text-sm leading-relaxed">The most common failure point. In extreme heat, the compressor works near maximum capacity continuously. Low refrigerant — often from a slow leak undetected through a cooler season — forces excessive cycling that overheats the clutch and internal components. When the compressor seizes, metal debris travels through the entire system, turning a single-component repair into a full system flush and replacement.</p>
  </div>
  <div class="rounded-xl p-5" style="background: #fef2f2; border-left: 4px solid #DC2626;">
    <h3 class="font-bold text-gray-900 mb-2">2. Condenser</h3>
    <p class="text-gray-700 text-sm leading-relaxed">The condenser tries to shed heat into air that's already 110°F+. Bent fins from road debris, clogged fins from insect accumulation and dust — extremely common in Arizona — all reduce airflow. A condenser operating below design efficiency pushes high-side pressures up, stressing every component downstream.</p>
  </div>
  <div class="rounded-xl p-5" style="background: #fef2f2; border-left: 4px solid #DC2626;">
    <h3 class="font-bold text-gray-900 mb-2">3. Refrigerant Lines and O-Rings</h3>
    <p class="text-gray-700 text-sm leading-relaxed">Phoenix's swing between cool overnight temps and blistering midday sun creates repeated thermal cycling stress. O-rings crack, shrink, and leak. Refrigerant lines develop micro-cracks at fittings. These leaks are gradual — the system keeps cooling "pretty well" right up until it doesn't, and the compressor has already paid the price for months of running low.</p>
  </div>
  <div class="rounded-xl p-5" style="background: #fef2f2; border-left: 4px solid #DC2626;">
    <h3 class="font-bold text-gray-900 mb-2">4. Expansion Valve and Receiver-Drier</h3>
    <p class="text-gray-700 text-sm leading-relaxed">Arizona's monsoon season spikes humidity into systems that are already thermally stressed. Once the receiver-drier's desiccant saturates, moisture causes acid formation that corrodes components and clogs the expansion valve — a part whose failure cascades into expensive compressor damage.</p>
  </div>
</div>

<h2 class="text-2xl font-bold text-gray-900 mb-6" style="border-left: 4px solid #DC2626; padding-left: 16px;">CT Shop's 5-Step Semi Truck AC Inspection</h2>
<div class="space-y-4 mb-12">
  <div class="flex gap-4 items-start rounded-xl p-5" style="background: #fafafa; border: 1px solid #e5e7eb;">

    <div><h3 class="font-bold text-gray-900 mb-1">System Pressure Test and Refrigerant Level Check</h3><p class="text-gray-700 text-sm leading-relaxed">Manifold gauges check both high-side and low-side pressures against OEM specs. Abnormal pressures flag compressor problems, condenser restrictions, or undercharge conditions before we go further.</p></div>

We trace the entire refrigerant loop, not just the most common failure points.</p></div>

<div class="rounded-2xl p-7 mb-12" style="background: linear-gradient(135deg, #1c1917 0%, #292524 100%); border: 1px solid #44403c;">
  <div class="flex items-center gap-3 mb-4"><span class="text-2xl">⚠️</span><h3 class="text-xl font-bold text-white">The Compounding Effect: Why Small AC Problems Become Big Bills in Phoenix</h3></div>
  <p class="text-gray-300 leading-relaxed mb-4">A slow refrigerant leak reduces system efficiency — so the compressor runs longer and hotter. That thermal stress degrades the clutch and seals. Meanwhile, reduced refrigerant charge means less lubricating oil circulating, so internal wear accelerates. By the time the driver notices "it's not quite as cold," the compressor is in late-stage distress.</p>
  <p style="color: #F59E0B;" class="font-semibold leading-relaxed">The original leak? Probably a $150–$300 O-ring replacement. The full downstream repair: easily 8–12× that cost. In Phoenix's heat, there is no "we'll keep an eye on it." Small AC faults compound fast.</p>
</div>

<h2 class="text-2xl font-bold text-gray-900 mb-6" style="border-left: 4px solid #DC2626; padding-left: 16px;">Common Mistakes Fleet Managers Make With AC in Arizona Heat</h2>
<div class="space-y-5 mb-12">
  <div class="rounded-xl p-5" style="background: #fffbeb; border-left: 4px solid #F59E0B;"><h3 class="font-bold text-gray-900 mb-2">Scheduling AC Inspections After Summer Starts</h3><p class="text-gray-600 text-sm leading-relaxed">By the time Phoenix hits June, shop schedules are maxed out and your truck's system has already been running under stress for weeks. Pre-season inspections in March or April let you schedule repairs on your timeline — not at roadside emergency rates.</p></div>
  <div class="rounded-xl p-5" style="background: #fffbeb; border-left: 4px solid #F59E0B;"><h3 class="font-bold text-gray-900 mb-2">Treating AC as a Comfort Issue, Not a Safety Issue</h3><p class="text-gray-600 text-sm leading-relaxed">In Phoenix in July, a broken AC is a genuine driver health risk — heat exhaustion and heat stroke are documented hazards for commercial drivers. Fleet managers who deprioritize AC repairs also expose themselves to liability if a driver experiences a heat-related medical event.</p></div>
  <div class="rounded-xl p-5" style="background: #fffbeb; border-left: 4px solid #F59E0B;"><h3 class="font-bold text-gray-900 mb-2">Using Non-OEM Refrigerant or Improper Charge Amounts</h3><p class="text-gray-600 text-sm leading-relaxed">Modern semi truck AC systems are engineered for specific refrigerant types and precise charge weights. Wrong refrigerant or overcharging raises system pressures beyond design limits — accelerating the exact failures you're trying to avoid.</p></div>
  <div class="rounded-xl p-5" style="background: #fffbeb; border-left: 4px solid #F59E0B;"><h3 class="font-bold text-gray-900 mb-2">Not Replacing the Receiver-Drier After Opening the System</h3><p class="text-gray-600 text-sm leading-relaxed">Every time an AC system is opened for repair, atmospheric moisture enters. Skipping drier replacement after a compressor swap introduces moisture-related acid corrosion into the "repaired" system within months.</p></div>
</div>

<h2 class="text-2xl font-bold text-gray-900 mb-6" style="border-left: 4px solid #DC2626; padding-left: 16px;">Pro Strategies for Keeping Semi Truck AC Running Through an Arizona Summer</h2>

<p class="text-gray-700 leading-relaxed mb-6">Add a dedicated AC inspection to your Q1 preventive maintenance rotation — ideally February through April for Phoenix-area trucks. Include pressure testing, leak check, condenser cleaning, and refrigerant charge verification. Address any findings before May. This single habit eliminates the majority of mid-summer AC emergencies in commercial fleets.</p>
