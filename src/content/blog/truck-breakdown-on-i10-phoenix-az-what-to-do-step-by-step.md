<h2 class="text-2xl font-bold text-gray-900 mb-2" style="border-left: 4px solid #DC2626; padding-left: 16px;">The 5-Step Breakdown Protocol for I-10 &amp; I-17</h2>
<p class="text-gray-600 mb-8">Follow these in order. Skipping steps one and two — even for 90 seconds — is how drivers get hurt.</p>

      <div><h3 class="text-gray-900 font-bold text-lg mb-2">Get Safe — Right Shoulder, All the Way Over</h3><p class="text-gray-700 text-sm leading-relaxed">The moment you sense trouble — loss of power, blowout, overheating — signal right and decelerate steadily. Your goal is maximum separation from live lanes. Once stopped, activate all four-way hazards immediately. Place reflective triangles or flares at 100 ft, 200 ft, and 300 ft behind the truck within the first two minutes — before any phone calls.</p></div>

<h2 class="text-2xl font-bold text-gray-900 mb-6" style="border-left: 4px solid #DC2626; padding-left: 16px;">Common Mistakes Phoenix Drivers Make During a Breakdown</h2>
<div class="grid md:grid-cols-2 gap-4 mb-12">
  <div class="rounded-xl p-5" style="background: #fffbeb; border-left: 4px solid #F59E0B;"><h3 class="font-bold text-gray-900 mb-2">Mistake #1: Calling 911 First for a Non-Emergency</h3><p class="text-gray-600 text-sm">911 is for life safety. ADOT's freeway service patrol handles some assists but cannot repair your truck. Call your repair service first.</p></div>
  <div class="rounded-xl p-5" style="background: #fffbeb; border-left: 4px solid #F59E0B;"><h3 class="font-bold text-gray-900 mb-2">Mistake #2: Accepting the First Tow Offer That Pulls Up</h3><p class="text-gray-600 text-sm">Opportunistic tow operators circle I-10 and I-17. Some haul rigs to lots with inflated storage fees. Only authorize a tow from a company you called.</p></div>
  <div class="rounded-xl p-5" style="background: #fffbeb; border-left: 4px solid #F59E0B;"><h3 class="font-bold text-gray-900 mb-2">Mistake #3: Trying to Limp to the Next Exit</h3><p class="text-gray-600 text-sm">Driving on a failing engine or blown tire regularly turns an $800 repair into a $5,000+ job. If the truck is telling you to stop, stop.</p></div>
  <div class="rounded-xl p-5" style="background: #fffbeb; border-left: 4px solid #F59E0B;"><h3 class="font-bold text-gray-900 mb-2">Mistake #4: Not Notifying Your Dispatcher Immediately</h3><p class="text-gray-600 text-sm">Carriers who notify shippers within 30 minutes almost always protect their relationship. Carriers who go silent for 4 hours while "handling it" often don't.</p></div>
  <div class="rounded-xl p-5" style="background: #fffbeb; border-left: 4px solid #F59E0B;"><h3 class="font-bold text-gray-900 mb-2">Mistake #5: Leaving Without a Written Work Order</h3><p class="text-gray-600 text-sm">Verbal repair agreements are unenforceable. If the repair fails 200 miles down the road, you need a written ticket for warranty claims and insurance reimbursement.</p></div>
  <div class="rounded-xl p-5" style="background: #fffbeb; border-left: 4px solid #F59E0B;"><h3 class="font-bold text-gray-900 mb-2">Mistake #6: Ignoring Heat-Related Secondary Damage</h3><p class="text-gray-600 text-sm">AZ summer heat accelerates every failure mode: coolant hose brittleness, tire sidewall fatigue, electrical connector oxidation. A shop that doesn't account for this will miss the root cause.</p></div>
</div>
